Introduction

Background Research:

RuPauls Drag Race premiered on LogoTv in 2009 and both produced and hosted by RuPaul a famous drag queen (queen of drag). Each season, 9-16 queens come to compete for some sort of prize ranging from cash to a lifetime supply of cosmetics. Each episode captures one week of prizes and a new celebrity judge. Each week features a mini-challenge at the beginning of the episode, main challenge judged by the judges, and a lipsync battle between the bottom two queens whose winner is determined by RuPaul herself. Mini-challenges consist of a smaller challenge such as a read-off which is similar to a roast. The mini-challenges are judged by only RuPaul and the winner receives immunity in the next weeks challenge. Main challenges consist of makeovers, singing challenges, and sewing challenges and are judged by the panel of judges. The main challenge highlights the winner of the challenge who receives immunity in the next weeks challenge, and the queens that will be competing in the lipsync battle. The loser of the lipsync battle gets eliminated. Drag Race has had 15 seasons so far, not including 6 spin-off series and international adaptations.

Project Objective:

Through our exploration of the data set, we will be able to gain insight on the subconscious biases that the show has, both informing the viewer - allowing them to have a greater understanding of the media that they consume and informing the show-runners- allowing them to analyze their productions biases potentially altering the show for greater equality should that be a concern.

Research questions:

  1. Can we predict the winner of RuPauls Drag Race using existing data and if so, how early can we do it?
  2. Does the age of the queen matter in the winners?

Hypothesis:

Younger contestants on Drag Race tend to win more on the show than older contestants. - Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)): There is no correlation between the age of a contestant and their likelihood to win. - Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_A\)): There younger the contestant, the greater their likelihood to be married is.

Data Description:

Our data is in the form of three dataframes. The first is rpdr_contep which stands for: RuPauls Drag Race Episode-Contestant Data it the contestant-level overview of the data and has 11 variables including season (vector of numerical variables), rank(vector of numerical variables), contestant(vector of categorical variables), and episode number(vector of numerical variables). The second table is rpdr_contestants which stands for: RuPauls Drag Race Contestant Data which are contestant-level data for RuPauls Drag Race for all available seasons this includes season, contestant, age, dob, and hometown. The third dataframe is rpdr_ep which stands for: RuPauls Drag Race Episode Data and includes episode-level data for RuPauls Drag Race for all available seasons. The dataframe has 22 variables including season, episode, airdate, numqueens, and lipsyncsong. An in-depth description of all variables can be found in the documentation: https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/dragracer/dragracer.pdf.

To perform a greater statistical analysis, we want to first see if there is variation between the means of the seasons. To do this we will perform an ANVOA test where our hypothesis are \(H_0\): There is no variability between the mean ages of each season and \(H_A\): There is at least one abnoral mean age in our dataset.

#perform the anova test with age for each season
anova_one_way <- aov(age~season, data = rpdr_contestants)
summary(anova_one_way)
##              Df Sum Sq Mean Sq F value Pr(>F)
## season       13    251   19.28   0.626   0.83
## Residuals   170   5235   30.79

As seen above, we have performed an ANOVA test. Because our test resulted in a p-value of 0.83, we fail to reject our null hypothesis, and continue to say that were 99% confident that there is no bias in any individual season. Since there is no bias an a season, we will continue to perform a 2 sample t-test between the winner data and the loser data. For our 2 sample t-test, we will first display the winner and loser data in a shared box plot. Then, we will continue our t-test where our hypothesis are \(H_0\): The true difference in the mean age of winners and loser is 0 and \(H_A\): The true difference in the mean age of winners and loser is not 0.

#columns: name, season, win(y/n)

#separate the winner and loser data
winners <- winner_data[order(winner_data$age, decreasing = FALSE),]
losers <- subset(ranked, rank != "1")

#display boxplots of the information
boxplot(winners$age, losers$age, main = "Ages of Winners and Losers",
ylab = "age",names = c("winners", "losers"))

#barplot(cyl_freq, horiz = TRUE)
t.test(winners$age, losers$age, alternative= "two.sided", paired = FALSE)

Since our t-test recieved a p-valuse of 0.7332 which is greater than 0.05, we fail to reject our null hypothesis that the true difference in means is equal to 0. As a result, we can conclude with 95% confidence that age is not a primary indicator in contestant winnings.

Conclusion

Anecdotally, younger contestants win more often than older contestants. Of the fourteen seasons of the show 11 of the seasons the winning Queen was younger than the mean age of contestants. There is no definitive reason that younger contestants win more often. However a few reasons why may be because younger contestants may be more attuned in the current fashion, music, and references throughout the competition. They are closer to the age of the target audience of the show. As seasons progress the younger queens have also grown up watching the show which may give them a better advantage as to how to do well and win on the show.

Our hypothesis was that younger contestants on Drag Race tend to win more on the show than older contestants. Despite the initial statistics and observations, our statistical test showed that age is not a signifying factor in the winners and losers. Therefore, our analysis does not support our hypothesis and we can conclude that the age of the drag queen is not a determining factor in their winnings.
