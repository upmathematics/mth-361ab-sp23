METHODS:

Twitter is a social media networking site where users can post 280-character messages, called tweets. These tweets are commonly accompanied by hashtags which are used as a self-categorization of the emotions or subjects contained therein. The hashtag used in a tweet often indicates the topic of a tweet or the emotions felt by the author. Its popularity amongst members of various demographic groups, combined with the the high volume of tweets produced each and every day (Mohammad 2012), makes it a natural tool for collecting training data. Furthermore, the usage of hashtags allows researchers to obtain self-classified data, enabling more efficient and accurate data collection.

All tweets were downloaded from December 2019 to July 2020. Preprocessing was done in Python to remove tweets in languages other than English. Additional processing was done to remove retweets and replies as these tweets tended to have a high degree of similarity that would lead to model over-fitting. The full training and testing dataset appears in the Appendix A. The module Python Data Analysis Library, or “pandas” was used to read and process the data (McKinney, 2010).

Hashtags were found for each of the eight variables. However, due to the binary nature of most of the classification algorithms, opposite characteristics (with opposite valence) were established for all of the variables if not already included in the dataset. For example, a control characteristic called ”happiness” was established as an opposite for characteristics such as ”desire for death”, or ”acquired capability”.

Establishing an opposite for each variable allows for the avoidance of more complex procedures and enables more realistic outputs. In the context of identifying potentially suicidal individuals, a binary classification scheme is logical given the natural overlap between characteristics. Classification of one characteristic against all other characteristics is considerably more arduous than a simple binary classification. This stems from the fact that multi-class problems classify items in a “one versus rest” structure, whereas binary classification problems are solely concerned with “one versus one” (Bishop, 2006).

Treating the characteristic emotions as binary becomes even more critical when considering the potential overlap between characteristics emotions that are not clear opposites. Text may also exhibit more than one characteristic, which a binary classification system allows for when opposite or control variables are introduced. Classification in a standard “one vs all”, or multi-class system additionally does not easily allow for interlocking classes or interdependent probabilities (Kourdi, 2004).

Processing the raw data is an essential step to training a sentiment analysis model. The data was split into testing and training subsets for cross-validation before any preprocessing was enacted to prevent data leakage. (cite Applied Multivariate Statistical Analysis, 1982) The corpus of tweets was pre-processed using sklearn and natural language processing toolkit (NLTK) tools. All letters were converted to lowercase, stop-words such as “the” and “an” were removed using an existing sklearn corpus, and all punctuation was removed. Unnatural letter repetitions were removed from the dataset to remove URLs from the data. Hashtagged words were removed from the dataset to prevent the classification algorithms from using the hashtag to classify tweets during training.

Count vectorization, also known as a “bag of words” model, creates a matrix containing all of the words in a corpus and counts the number of times each term occurs in the matrix (L ̈ubbering, 2018). This method is often unable to analyze complicated relationships within phrases, but by using a built-in “ngram” parameter provided by sklearn, a solution to this problem is provided by grouping phrases of an optimal length together. (Pedregosa 2011).

Algorithms tested include a random forest classifier, linear support vector machine (SVM), a non-linear support vector classifier (SVC), logistic regression classifier, and a Multinomial Naıve Bayes classifier. These algorithms were tested using data tokenized with both TD-IDF and count vectors, but boasted a unanimously higher f1 score using count vectors. For all variables, existing data was divided into a training and testing set in an 90/10 ratio. An f-score was derived from each algorithm’s respective performance with the testing set (Huang). The use of the f-score eliminated any concerns over unbalanced classes contributing to a falsely high accuracy score. An abbreviated overview of each algorithm’s performance can be found above.

The f-score is a measure of algorithm performance that is not susceptible to bias due to unbalanced classes. Whereas a simple score such as proportion of correct classifications (ie "accuracy") may be biased by data with unequal sizes of the two classes, the f-score provides a robust score of binary classifier accuracy. The f-score is defined as the harmonic mean of precision and recall. Precision is a measure of true positive class predictions, whereas recall is a measure of the ratio of true positive class predictions to total positive class data. Thus, f-score provides a metric roughly analogous in application to p-values and a metric based in the idea of Type I and Type II errors.

Platt scaling was used to derive a probabilistic or continuous value from the discrete classification data using a sigmoid function. These values allowed the study of quantitative emotional changes over time - rather than simply examining whether or not studied document demonstrates an emotion or sentiment. Thus, Platt scaling "hacks" the binary classifier into becoming a continuous classifier through the use of isotonic regression.

SUPPORT VECTOR MACHINES:

The most robust model proved to be a support vector machine with a radial basis function kernel. Support vector machines are non-probabilistic binary linear classifiers which map data to points in an arbitrary space. Clusterings of data are separated by a canonical hyperplane, which is defined by support vectors. Support vectors are pairs of data which are as close together as possible without crossing the decision hyperplane. This distance between support vectors is called the margin. The farther a data point is from the decision hyperplane, the greater the confidence in an accurate classification. Therefore, the objective of an SVM model is to maximize the margin. The optimal decision hyperplane is formed by reducing the number of nonzero weights, or support vectors, to maximize the margin between clusters. Results and Discussions

Obtaining raw output values from the sentiment analysis models is only one step in the process of performing a proper quantitative analysis. Since the aim is to study the life of Vincent Van Gogh, particularly his suicide, through the lens of mathematical modelling, further statistical analysis is necessary.

Critical slowing down, though a predictor of suicide, can be difficult to detect. Studies in the fields of nonlinear dynamics show that statistical metrics such as autocorrelation, kurtosis, and skew are metrics serving as an early warning sign of critical showing down - or, in this context, of suicide (Lenton, 2012). Thus, a spike in the autocorrelation, kurtosis, or skew of an emotion over time serves as an early warning sign of suicidality. Simultaneous spikes in two or more of the metrics increase the likelihood of impending phase change dramatically by backing the validity of the predictive power of each metric.

Variance and skewness are widely supported by literature as indicators of critical slowing down (Leemput, 2014), and as such were the metrics used to analyse the time series data. Skewness (5) is defined as the distortion from data distribution, using the Fisher-Pearson Standardized Moment Coefficient (Cite Doane 2011). Variance (6) is defined as variability from the mean (Akman, 2011). Autocorrelation is a measure representing the ”inertia” of sentiments over time.

Analyzing suicidality at an individual or even population level is incredibly difficult due to a myriad of legal, ethical, and logistical barriers. The entire project was born out of a weakness of a traditional discipline. Though psychology, through the subfield of suicidology, enabled the theoretical groundwork for this project, examining the validity of psychological theory of suicide in practice was difficult due to the multitude of ethical, logistical, and legal barriers preventing the necessary experiments or data acquisition. Thus, in order to get data to evaluate the veracity of the psychological theory - namely that critical slowing down serves as an early warning to suicide - another discipline had to be introduced.

I then used sentiment analysis as a complementary research tool, enabling me to further study the psychology of suicide through a mathematical lens. Sentiment analysis and machine learning offer tools to blunt the difficulty posed by the problem of studying suicide by allowing for the conversion of qualitative data into quantitative data usable for time series analysis and other forms of quantitative methodology. This approach was admittedly far from perfect - assigning a quantitative score to something as complex as writing is reductive. However, any sort of modeling is, by definition, reductive. The sentiment analysis approach was the most feasible and cleanest possible solution given legal and ethical constraints barring other forms of data collection.

The insight afforded to us through the use of sentiment analysis provides a valuable lens into the mindset and life of the famed artist.
